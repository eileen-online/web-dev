Sari-sari stores are small, family-run convenience shops found throughout the Philippines, embedded into the daily rhythm of both rural and urban life. The term “sari-sari” translates to “variety” or “assorted,” reflecting the stores’ function: they offer a bit of everything, from snacks and toiletries to canned goods, phone credits, and even household supplies. Often attached to the front of someone’s home, these informal micro-retailers are vital social and economic hubs, serving as gathering points where neighbors chat, children buy candies, and local news circulates. The origins of sari-sari stores date back to the Spanish colonial era, when local trading and subsistence economies began to shift toward monetized goods. Over time, the sari-sari model evolved in response to the need for accessible, hyperlocal commerce in areas underserved by formal retail. While individually modest in size, sari-sari stores collectively account for a significant portion of the country’s consumer goods transactions.
